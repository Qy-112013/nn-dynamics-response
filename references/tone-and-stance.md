# Tone and stance

The house voice is **warm, deferential, and concrete**. Every reply acknowledges the reviewer
first, then states the action, then points to the location. Gratitude is generous and sincere;
disagreement is rare, polite, and always backed by a scientific or scope reason.

## The reply arc (use for almost every point)

1. **Thank / acknowledge** — "Thanks the reviewer for …" (vary the opener; see phrase-bank.md).
2. **Action** — "According to your comment, we have …" / "In response, we have …".
3. **Locate** — "(please see page N of Section X in the revised version for details)."
4. (If applicable) **Paste** the author-supplied revised text in `\textcolor{blue}{…}`.

## Recommended language

- Acknowledge value: "encouraging comments", "constructive suggestion", "insightful question",
  "valuable comment", "careful observation".
- Take responsibility for misunderstandings: if a reviewer misread the manuscript, first
  consider whether the presentation caused it — "we have clarified …", "we apologize for the
  oversight" — rather than "the reviewer misunderstood".
- Concede generously when the reviewer is right or raises a more general case: "We completely
  agree that …".

## Disagreement (rare, careful)

When you must push back:

1. Acknowledge the concern first.
2. Give a **scientific or scope-based** reason (often as a new Remark in the manuscript).
3. Offer what you *can* do (a clarification, a partial analysis, a future-work note).

Reviewer-suggested citations are the most common pushback. House move: evaluate each, cite the
genuinely relevant ones with explicit justification, and decline the rest politely — and you
may cite the editor's own guidance that irrelevant citations need not be added. See the
"Polite disagreement & reviewer-suggested citations" block in phrase-bank.md.

## Acceptance rounds

When a reviewer recommends acceptance, keep it short and gracious — do not over-explain:
> We sincerely thank the reviewer for the positive assessment and are pleased that the paper is deemed acceptable for publication.

## Forbidden

- Hostile, defensive, or accusatory language; "the reviewer is wrong/misunderstood".
- Refusing a requested experiment/analysis on grounds of **time, money, or convenience**. If
  it is genuinely out of scope, say so on scientific/scope grounds and add a future-work note.
- Hiding a limitation or overclaiming a change that was not actually made.
- Sarcasm, exclamation marks, or hype.
- Dismissing a comment without a substantive response.
